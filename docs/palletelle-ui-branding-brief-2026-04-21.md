# Palletelle UI Branding Brief

## Objective
Define a build-ready brand direction for Palletelle across storefront and admin so the product feels elegant, polished, modern, cohesive, and premium without becoming ornate, precious, or lifestyle-fluffy.

This brief is for implementation. It is not a mood board.

## Brand position to implement
Palletelle should feel like a disciplined, quietly premium style house.

Target impression:
- refined
- calm
- curated
- competent
- feminine without fragility
- modern without tech-brightness
- premium without luxury theater

Avoid:
- glossy fashion-mag drama
- wellness-brand softness
- boho craft cues
- aggressive DTC conversion styling
- generic SaaS dashboard chrome
- overexplaining the product flow in primary brand moments

## Core UI principle
The brand should present guidance as considered curation, not software process.

In practice:
- lead with taste, then utility
- use fewer, stronger sections
- let whitespace and typography carry status
- keep forms and controls calm and efficient
- make trust and subscription messaging feel composed, not salesy

## Visual direction
## Overall aesthetic
Use an editorial-retail shell with product-grade clarity.

The UI should feel:
- warm-light, not stark white
- structured, not busy
- spacious, not sparse for the sake of it
- tactile, not glossy
- confident, not loud

## Layout behavior
- Prefer wide margins and stable content rails.
- Use a clear max-width system: hero/content at 1200 to 1280px, reading zones at 640 to 720px.
- Default to 12-column desktop layout, 6-column tablet, 4-column mobile.
- Use asymmetry sparingly, mostly in hero and editorial modules.
- Avoid stacking many equal cards near the top of pages.

## Surface hierarchy
Use only four surface levels:
1. Base canvas
2. Raised panel
3. Quiet tinted panel
4. Inverted dark panel

Do not invent page-specific surface treatments. Reuse these consistently.

## Typography direction
Use a two-family system.

### 1. Editorial serif
Use for:
- hero headlines
- collection names
- premium membership headings
- selected pull quotes and sectional moments

Desired traits:
- modern serif
- moderate contrast, not hairline-thin
- elegant but sturdy
- clean terminals, no decorative swashes

Good implementation direction:
- Canela / Cormorant Garamond is too romantic unless tightly controlled
- prefer something closer to Freight Display, Austin, or a modern editorial serif with restraint
- if licensing is a constraint, choose a high-quality Google-adjacent serif with calm contrast and good italics

### 2. UI sans
Use for:
- body copy
- navigation
- filters
- forms
- badges
- admin
- product facts and prices

Desired traits:
- neutral-warm
- readable at small sizes
- not geometric-techy
- not overly humanist or soft

Implementation recommendation:
- use one sans across storefront and admin
- weights: 400, 500, 600 only
- avoid mixing multiple sans families

## Type scale
- Hero display: 56 to 72px desktop, 40 to 48px mobile
- H1: 40 to 48px desktop, 32px mobile
- H2: 28 to 32px
- H3: 20 to 24px
- Body large: 18px/28px
- Body default: 16px/24px
- Meta/labels: 12 to 13px with controlled tracking

## Type behavior
- Headings use tighter copy blocks and more line discipline.
- Body copy stays short. Rarely exceed 3 lines per paragraph in key commerce sections.
- Use uppercase only for micro-labels, step labels, and overlines.
- Track uppercase labels slightly wide, but do not make the whole UI look labeled to death.

## Color and material direction
Use a restrained warm-neutral palette with one wine-toned signature accent and one olive-sage utility accent.

## Core palette roles
### Base
- Canvas: warm parchment, not pure white
- Panel: soft ivory
- Subtle section tint: stone-beige

### Text
- Primary text: deep ink-brown
- Secondary text: softened ink
- Divider/border: warm grey at low contrast

### Signature accent
- Rosewood / muted plum-brown for primary CTAs, selected highlights, active states, and premium cues

### Secondary accent
- Olive-sage for filters, fit/profile cues, and natural intelligence notes

### Dark mode panel, not full site dark mode
- Espresso-plum for inverted sections, premium membership blocks, strong footer, and selected admin chrome

### Metallic/material cue
- No literal gold gradients
- If a premium highlight is needed, use a dry brass tone in illustration, rules, or icon accent only

## Usage rules
- 70 percent warm neutral base
- 20 percent ink and dark support
- 10 percent accent max
- Keep rosewood sparse so it keeps authority
- Avoid bright green success tones unless functionally necessary
- Avoid cool blue unless absolutely required for system messaging

## Spacing and component feel
## Spacing rhythm
Use an 8px system with generous page intervals.

Recommended page spacing:
- page top padding: 48 to 72px desktop
- section spacing: 72 to 120px desktop, 48 to 72px mobile
- card internal padding: 20 to 28px
- form group spacing: 16 to 24px

## Corner radius
- Primary cards and inputs: 16px
- Compact chips and pills: 999px
- Large hero/media modules: 24px

Do not mix many radii. The product should feel settled.

## Borders and shadows
- Default to 1px warm low-contrast borders
- Use shadows lightly and softly, mostly vertical with broad blur
- Prefer contrast through surface color and spacing over heavy shadows

## Motion
- Use short, quiet transitions: 160 to 220ms
- Hover should feel like refinement, not jumpiness
- Prefer opacity, border, shadow, and 1 to 2px translate adjustments
- No springy marketing animations in core commerce flow

## CTA style
## Primary CTA
- Solid rosewood button
- White or warm-ivory text
- Height 48 to 52px
- Radius 999px or 16px depending on placement, but pick one system-wide
- Weight 600
- No heavy gradients
- Hover: slightly deeper tone and subtle lift

Use for:
- Register
- Start profile
- Continue to recommendations
- Save membership choice
- Checkout-adjacent forward actions

## Secondary CTA
- Ink text with subtle border on ivory panel
- Hover adds light stone fill
- Same height as primary CTA

## Tertiary CTA
- Text link with underline-on-hover or thin trailing icon
- Reserved for less-committal actions only

## CTA copy direction
Buttons should sound composed and direct.

Use:
- Create account
- Build my palette
- View the edit
- Continue
- Compare plans

Avoid:
- Unlock magic
- Transform your style
- Get started now for free today

## Card and tile style
## Product cards
Product cards should read as premium retail cards, not feed widgets.

Specs:
- larger image-first ratio, ideally 4:5
- minimal persistent chrome
- price and product title aligned cleanly under image
- badges used sparingly
- favorite/save affordance should be quiet
- hover can reveal secondary info or quick action, but do not clutter resting state

## Editorial tiles
Use for collections, seasonal edits, guidance, and subscription explanation.

Specs:
- more breathing room than product cards
- optional eyebrow, heading, 2-line body max, single CTA
- can use tinted or dark surface backgrounds
- keep artwork/crops clean and intentional

## Functional cards
Use for account modules, saved items, plan details, and admin summaries.

Specs:
- flatter than editorial tiles
- stronger data alignment
- clearer row rhythm
- less decorative treatment

## Badge/chip system
Only three badge types:
- neutral meta
- olive informational
- rosewood premium

Do not create rainbow badge language.

## Premium membership and subscription treatment
Subscription should feel like a calm private-membership layer, not a loud upsell funnel.

## Premium visual treatment
- Use a dark espresso-plum or deep ink panel with warm text
- Pair with editorial serif heading and quiet sans details
- Introduce a subtle rule, monogram, or seal motif, not sparkles or glow
- Use one premium badge style consistently across homepage, account, and plan selection

## Premium copy behavior
Position membership as:
- deeper access
- earlier access
- more considered guidance
- a more tailored relationship with the brand

Do not position it as hype, scarcity theater, or fake exclusivity.

## Premium module structure
Each subscription block should contain:
1. clear title
2. one-sentence value summary
3. 3 to 5 concrete benefits
4. price and billing cadence in quiet UI sans
5. one primary CTA
6. concise trust note about cancellation or plan control

## Plan comparison styling
- one featured plan only
- featured plan gets premium surface or border emphasis
- comparison rows should be clean and readable, not marketing-colored
- billing note should sit close to price, never buried in tiny text

## Page-by-page application
## Homepage
Change first.

Structure:
1. refined hero with brand promise, strong image or composition, one primary CTA, one secondary CTA
2. curated value strip, 3 points max
3. featured edit or seasonal selection
4. how Palletelle works, simplified to 3 steps
5. premium membership or trust block
6. selected products or categories
7. final CTA

Rules:
- move operational explanation lower
- remove equal-weight utility cards from above the fold
- use one hero statement that sounds editorial, not technical

## Product listing and product detail pages
- tighten filter chrome, reduce visible border noise
- use whitespace to separate merchandising from filter controls
- product detail should prioritize imagery, fit guidance, material notes, and honest recommendation context
- keep trust labels factual and unobtrusive
- recommendation explanation should feel clear and measured, not algorithmic-showy

## Registration and onboarding
This area must be warmer and more premium than default auth screens.

Rules:
- split long onboarding into calm sequential steps
- keep one question focus per screen where possible
- explain why a field helps in one short line
- use progress indicator with understated styling
- avoid big card stacks and excessive form borders
- make optional fields visibly optional

## Subscription messaging
- integrate into natural moments: homepage, account, saved looks, preference refinement
- show premium as continuity and service quality, not interruption
- keep billing explanations plain and immediate

## Account area
The account should feel like a personal wardrobe desk.

Structure:
- overview
- profile and palette
- saved products and edits
- membership
- orders or purchase history
- settings

Style rules:
- cleaner density than homepage
- functional cards with strong alignment
- premium membership gets one elevated panel, not repeated upsells everywhere
- saved items should look collectible and orderly

## Checkout-adjacent experiences
Even if full checkout is not implemented yet, nearby surfaces should follow the same language.

Rules:
- remove visual noise and competing promos
- keep totals and plan choices calm and legible
- trust messages should sit near action point, not as dense footer clutter
- primary action must be visually obvious
- use premium cues only if membership materially changes the choice

## Admin application
Admin should feel like the same house in work mode.

## Admin brand rules
- same UI sans as storefront
- same color tokens, radii, borders, spacing rhythm
- same primary accent for selected states and actions
- darker header/nav is fine, but use the same espresso-plum/ink family
- dashboards should favor clear tables and modules over decorative boxes

## Admin should differ from storefront by
- denser information layout
- lower image emphasis
- stronger utility hierarchy
- fewer editorial serif moments

## Admin should still share
- typography logic
- button styling
- form styling
- chip/badge styling
- spacing rhythm
- page chrome and brand lockup behavior

## Implementation tokens to define first
Create and use shared design tokens before page redesign.

## Required token groups
- colors: canvas, panel, panel-subtle, text-primary, text-secondary, border, accent-primary, accent-secondary, accent-premium, success, warning, danger
- typography: font-serif, font-sans, text-display, text-h1, text-h2, text-h3, text-body, text-meta
- spacing: 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 72, 96
- radius: sm, md, lg, xl, pill
- shadow: soft, raised
- motion: fast, standard

## Component families to standardize immediately
1. buttons
2. text fields/selects
3. chips/badges
4. cards: product, editorial, functional
5. section headers
6. membership plan modules
7. tables/list rows for admin
8. empty states
9. modals/drawers

## What should change first
## Phase 1, immediate
1. Resolve final brand naming everywhere to Palletelle if that is the chosen customer-facing brand.
2. Build shared tokens across storefront and admin.
3. Replace current homepage structure with a stronger hero and fewer explanatory panels.
4. Standardize button, card, form, and badge patterns.

## Phase 2, next
5. Restyle registration and onboarding to match the new token system.
6. Update product listing and detail pages for calmer merchandising hierarchy.
7. Build premium membership modules and comparison blocks with one consistent visual treatment.
8. Bring admin nav, dashboard, tables, and forms onto the same brand tokens.

## Phase 3, polish
9. Add refined art direction rules for product crops, editorial imagery, and empty states.
10. Tune motion, hover states, and responsive spacing.
11. Introduce a compact monogram/brand mark treatment for header, account, and admin.

## Non-negotiables
- no pure black and pure white default pairing
- no bright promotional accent colors
- no gradients as core identity
- no box-heavy homepage grid above the fold
- no separate admin visual language
- no overly clever luxury copy
- no exaggerated personalization claims

## Success test
The redesign is correct if:
- homepage feels curated in under 3 seconds
- onboarding feels calm and considered, not procedural
- membership feels premium but not pushy
- product pages feel more like a refined retailer than a generic app
- admin feels related to storefront immediately
- the whole product can be described as quiet premium, not flashy premium

## Final recommendation
Implement Palletelle as a warm editorial commerce system with shared tokens across storefront and admin. The first move is not more decorative work, it is tighter hierarchy: stronger hero, fewer cards, better typography contrast, calmer forms, one premium membership treatment, and one unified design system used everywhere.
